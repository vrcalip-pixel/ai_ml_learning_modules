üéØ The Big Picture

All machine learning algorithms do the same basic thing:

  1. Look at examples (training data)
  2. Find patterns (what features matter?)
  3. Make predictions (apply patterns to new data)
  4. Improve over time (learn from mistakes)
Email Spam Filter: Instead of programming every possible spam word, ML algorithms look at thousands of emails you've marked as spam vs. not spam, find patterns (words like "FREE!" and "Click here!" appear often in spam), and use those patterns to predict if new emails are spam.

üå≥ Root Node

What it is: The first question the tree asks. This should be the question that best splits your data into meaningful groups.

Example: "Is the customer's purchase history > $500?" This splits customers into high-spenders and low-spenders.

How it's chosen: The algorithm tests every possible question and picks the one that creates the clearest separation.

What it is: Follow-up questions that further refine the decision. Each branch leads to more questions or a final answer.

Example: After the root asks about purchase history, a branch might ask "Did they click on our last email?"

Depth matters: Too many questions = overly complex (overfitting). Too few = too simple (underfitting).

üçÉ Leaf Node

What it is: The final decision or prediction. No more questions‚Äîthis is the answer!

Example: "YES, this customer will likely make a purchase" or "NO, they probably won't buy."

In classification: Assigns a category (spam/not spam). In regression: Predicts a number ($125 expected purchase).

Drawing a "line of best fit" through scattered points on a graph (like you did in math class!). If you know someone's hours studied and their test scores, you can draw a line to predict what score someone will get based on study hours.

How It Works (Simply Explained)

üìä The Mathematics (Simplified)
  1. Plot your data: Put all your examples on a graph (x-axis = input, y-axis = output)
  2. Draw a line: The algorithm finds the line that comes closest to all points
  3. Make predictions: For any new input, follow the line to find the predicted output
  4. Measure error: Check how far off predictions are and adjust the line

The Magic Formula: y = mx + b

  • y = what you're predicting (house price)
  • x = what you know (house size)
  • m = slope (how much y changes when x changes)
  • b = starting point (y-intercept)

The algorithm's job is to find the best values for m and b!

K-Nearest Neighbors: Birds of a Feather

K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) An algorithm that makes predictions by looking at the "K" most similar examples. It assumes that similar things exist close together‚Äîyou are like your neighbors!
Moving to a new neighborhood. To guess if you'll like it, you look at your closest neighbors (the "K" nearest). If 7 out of 10 neighbors love the area, you'll probably like it too. KNN does the same with data!

ü§î Choosing the Right K
  • K=1: Look at only the single closest neighbor
    • Pro: Very sensitive to local patterns
    • Con: Can be thrown off by outliers/noise
  • K=3 or K=5: Most common choices
    • Pro: Balanced‚Äînot too sensitive, not too broad
    • Con: Still somewhat influenced by local variations
  • Large K (like K=20): Look at many neighbors
    • Pro: Smoother predictions, less affected by noise
    • Con: Might miss important local patterns
Rule of thumb: Try K = ‚àö(number of data points). For 100 data points, start with K=10. Then test a few values nearby to see what works best!

üì• Input Layer

What it is: The entrance to the network where raw data comes in. Each "neuron" in this layer represents one feature of your data.

Example (House Price Prediction):

  • Neuron 1: Square footage
  • Neuron 2: Number of bedrooms
  • Neuron 3: Age of house
  • Neuron 4: Neighborhood rating

What happens: Each input gets a number (the feature value), which then flows through connections to the next layer.

üß† Hidden Layer(s)

What it is: The "thinking" layers where patterns are detected and combined. Deep networks have many hidden layers (that's why it's called "deep" learning!).

What they do: Each neuron looks at inputs, applies weights (importance), and decides whether to "fire" (activate) based on what it sees.

Example:

  • First hidden layer: Detects simple patterns ("large house", "many bedrooms", "old property")
  • Second hidden layer: Combines simple patterns into complex ones ("luxury home", "fixer-upper", "family-friendly")

The magic: The network learns which patterns matter through training! You don't program these‚Äîit discovers them from data.

üì§ Output Layer

What it is: The final layer that produces the prediction or classification.

For classification (categories): Each neuron represents one possible category. The one with the highest activation "wins."

  • Example: [Cat: 0.9, Dog: 0.05, Bird: 0.05] ‚Üí It's a cat!

For regression (numbers): Usually one neuron that outputs a predicted value.

  • Example: Output = $325,000 (predicted house price)

How Neural Networks Learn

üéì The Learning Process
  1. Forward Pass: Data flows through the network, layer by layer, to make a prediction
  2. Compare to Reality: Check how wrong the prediction was (the "error" or "loss")
  3. Backward Pass (Backpropagation): Work backwards through the network, adjusting the connections (weights) to reduce error
  4. Repeat: Do this thousands or millions of times with different examples
  5. Result: The network gets better and better at making accurate predictions!
Learning to throw darts. Your first throw misses badly (high error). Your brain adjusts your aim (weight adjustment). Next throw is closer. Your brain keeps adjusting until you consistently hit the bullseye. Neural networks do the same thing‚Äîjust with math instead of muscle memory!

Machine Learning Glossary

Quick reference for all the terms you learned in this module:

Algorithm: A step-by-step procedure for solving a problem or making predictions from data.
Backpropagation: The process neural networks use to learn‚Äîworking backwards to adjust connections based on errors.
Classification: Predicting which category something belongs to (spam/not spam, cat/dog/bird).
Clustering: Grouping similar items together without predefined categories.
CNN (Convolutional Neural Network): A type of neural network specialized for processing images and visual data.
Deep Learning: Machine learning using neural networks with many hidden layers (hence "deep").
Features: The input variables or characteristics used to make predictions (e.g., house size, age, location).
Hidden Layer: Layers between input and output in a neural network where pattern detection happens.
K: In K-Nearest Neighbors, the number of closest neighbors to consider when making a prediction.
Label: The correct answer in training data (what you're trying to predict).
Loss/Error: How wrong the model's predictions are; learning aims to minimize this.
Neuron: A basic unit in a neural network that receives inputs, processes them, and passes output to the next layer.
Overfitting: When a model learns the training data TOO well and performs poorly on new data (memorizes instead of learns).
Regression: Predicting a continuous number (price, temperature, sales).
RNN (Recurrent Neural Network): A neural network type for sequential data like text or time series.
Training: The process of feeding data to an algorithm so it can learn patterns and improve predictions.
Transformer: A modern neural network architecture that powers language models like ChatGPT.
Weights: Numbers that determine the strength of connections between neurons; these get adjusted during learning.

Decision Trees in Business

Why Businesses Love Decision Trees

Decision trees are among the most popular algorithms in business because they're easy to explain to non-technical stakeholders. When your boss asks "Why did the model make that decision?", you can literally show them the tree!

üìä 73% of Fortune 500 companies use decision trees in at least one business process

Real Business Application: Customer Churn Prediction

Telecom Company Case Study:

A major cell phone carrier wanted to predict which customers would cancel their service. They built a decision tree that asked:

  1. Root Question: "Has customer called support more than 3 times this month?"
    • Yes ‚Üí High churn risk (proceed to next question)
    • No ‚Üí Low churn risk (keep monitoring)
  2. Second Question: "Is their monthly bill above $80?"
    • Yes ‚Üí Very high risk ‚Üí Action: Offer retention discount
    • No ‚Üí Medium risk ‚Üí Action: Send satisfaction survey

Result: Reduced churn by 18% and saved $12M annually by targeting the right customers with the right offers.

More Business Applications

  • Credit Approval: Banks use decision trees to approve/deny loans based on income, credit score, debt ratio, employment history
  • Medical Diagnosis: Hospitals use them for triage‚Äî"Does patient have fever?" ‚Üí "Above 102¬∞F?" ‚Üí "Difficulty breathing?" ‚Üí Treatment path
  • Marketing Segmentation: E-commerce sites classify customers into "deal seekers", "brand loyal", "impulse buyers" to target messaging
  • Fraud Detection: Credit card companies identify suspicious transactions through decision tree patterns
When presenting to executives, show them the decision tree visually. They'll understand it immediately‚Äîmuch better than trying to explain neural network weights!

Advanced Concept: Ensemble Methods

Single decision trees can be unstable (small data changes = different tree). The solution? Random Forests‚Äîcreate hundreds of decision trees, each trained on slightly different data, then vote on the answer.

Think of it as: Instead of asking one expert, you ask 100 experts and go with the majority opinion. Much more reliable!

Common Pitfalls & Solutions

  • Problem: Tree becomes too deep and complex (overfitting)
    • Solution: Set maximum depth (e.g., no more than 5 levels of questions)
  • Problem: Biased toward features with many possible values
    • Solution: Use algorithms like C4.5 or CART that compensate for this
  • Problem: Doesn't handle continuous relationships well (curves)
    • Solution: Consider gradient boosted trees or neural networks for complex relationships

Regression in Business

The Power of Prediction

Every business needs to predict numbers: sales, costs, demand, prices. Linear regression is often the first algorithm data scientists try because it's fast, interpretable, and surprisingly effective for many problems.

üí∞ Companies using predictive analytics (like regression) are 2.9x more likely to report revenue growth above their industry average

Real Application: Dynamic Pricing at Uber

Uber uses regression models to predict demand and set "surge pricing":

  • Inputs: Time of day, day of week, weather, local events, historical demand
  • Model: Multiple linear regression predicts expected ride requests per minute
  • Output: If predicted demand > available drivers, increase prices to balance supply/demand
  • Impact: Ensures riders can always get rides when they need them (drivers attracted by higher fares)

Common Business Use Cases

  • Sales Forecasting: Predict next quarter's revenue based on marketing spend, seasonality, economic indicators
  • Inventory Planning: Forecast product demand to optimize stock levels (not too much, not too little)
  • Real Estate Valuation: Zillow's "Zestimate" uses regression on square footage, beds, baths, location, recent sales
  • Ad Spend Optimization: Predict ROI for each marketing channel to allocate budget effectively
  • Employee Retention: Predict tenure based on salary, role, manager rating, career growth opportunities
Always visualize your regression! Plot actual values vs. predicted values. If points are all over the place, your model needs improvement. If they follow the diagonal line closely, you're doing well!

Beyond Simple Linear Regression

Multiple Regression: More than one input variable (e.g., predict house price from size + bedrooms + age + location)

Polynomial Regression: Captures curved relationships, not just straight lines

Ridge/Lasso Regression: Prevents overfitting when you have lots of input features

When Regression Fails

  • Non-linear relationships: If the true relationship is curved or complex, try polynomial regression or neural networks
  • Outliers: A few extreme values can throw off the entire line‚Äîclean your data first!
  • Categorical predictions: Regression predicts numbers; if you need categories (spam/not spam), use classification algorithms instead

Measuring Success: R¬≤ Score

R¬≤ (R-squared) tells you how well your line fits the data:

  • R¬≤ = 1.0: Perfect fit (every point on the line) ‚Äî suspiciously good, probably overfit!
  • R¬≤ = 0.7-0.9: Strong relationship, useful predictions
  • R¬≤ = 0.4-0.6: Moderate relationship, some predictive value
  • R¬≤ < 0.3: Weak relationship, predictions won't be very accurate

K-Nearest Neighbors in Action

The "Show Me Similar Examples" Algorithm

KNN is powerful because it's intuitive: find similar past cases and predict based on what happened to them. No complex math‚Äîjust similarity and voting!

üéØ KNN powers 40%+ of recommendation systems in e-commerce and streaming services

E-commerce: Amazon's "Customers Also Bought"

When you view a product, Amazon uses a variant of KNN:

  1. Finds customers similar to you (based on browsing/purchase history)
  2. Looks at what those similar customers bought
  3. Recommends the most popular items among your "neighbors"

Result: 35% of Amazon's revenue comes from recommendations‚Äîthat's $160 billion annually!

Healthcare: Personalized Treatment Recommendations

Hospitals use KNN to recommend treatments:

  • Input: New patient with symptoms: age 45, diabetic, high blood pressure, chest pain
  • KNN Process: Find 10 most similar past patients (same age range, conditions, symptoms)
  • Output: 8 out of 10 similar patients responded well to Treatment A ‚Üí Recommend Treatment A
  • Benefit: Evidence-based recommendations from real patient outcomes, not just textbook guidelines

More Applications

  • Content Recommendation: Netflix finds users with similar viewing patterns and recommends their favorites
  • Real Estate Comparables: "Homes similar to this one sold for $X" uses KNN to find comparable properties
  • Fraud Detection: Compare new transaction to similar past transactions‚Äîif neighbors were fraudulent, flag it!
  • Image Recognition: "This new image looks like training images labeled 'cat'" ‚Üí Predict cat
Feature scaling is critical for KNN! If one feature (income: 0-200,000) has much larger range than another (age: 0-100), it will dominate distance calculations. Always normalize your features to 0-1 range.

Advantages & Disadvantages

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Simple to understand and implement
  • No training phase required (just store data)
  • Naturally handles multi-class problems
  • Adapts as new data arrives

‚ùå Disadvantages:

  • Slow for large datasets (must calculate distance to every point!)
  • Sensitive to irrelevant features (noise can mislead distance calculation)
  • Struggles with high-dimensional data (curse of dimensionality)
  • Memory intensive (must store all training data)

Optimizing KNN Performance

  • Choose K wisely: Try odd numbers (3, 5, 7, 9) to avoid ties in voting
  • Use distance weighting: Closer neighbors get more votes than distant ones
  • Feature selection: Remove irrelevant features that add noise
  • Use approximate methods: For huge datasets, use algorithms like LSH (Locality-Sensitive Hashing) to find neighbors faster

Neural Networks Deep Dive

The Foundation of Modern AI

Neural networks are behind the AI revolution: self-driving cars, facial recognition, language translation, ChatGPT, DALL-E. Understanding them is essential for any AI practitioner.

üöÄ Investment in deep learning (neural networks) grew from $1B in 2015 to $50B+ in 2024

How They Actually Work: Step-by-Step

1. Initialization: Network starts with random weights (connections between neurons)

2. Forward Pass:

  • Input data enters the network (e.g., image pixels)
  • Each neuron: multiplies inputs by weights, adds them up, applies activation function
  • Data flows layer by layer until reaching output
  • Output is the prediction (e.g., "This is a cat")

3. Calculate Error: Compare prediction to correct answer (how wrong were we?)

4. Backward Pass (Backpropagation):

  • Work backwards through network
  • Calculate how much each weight contributed to the error
  • Adjust weights to reduce error (learning!)

5. Repeat: Do this millions of times with different examples until error is minimized

Activation Functions: The "On/Off Switch"

Activation functions decide whether a neuron "fires" or not. Common ones:

  • ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit): Most popular. If input negative ‚Üí output 0, else output = input. Simple and effective!
  • Sigmoid: Squashes output to 0-1 range. Good for probabilities.
  • Tanh: Similar to sigmoid but outputs -1 to 1
  • Softmax: Used in output layer for multi-class classification (converts scores to probabilities that sum to 1)

Business Application: Fraud Detection at PayPal

PayPal processes 19 million transactions daily. Neural networks analyze:

  • Transaction amount, location, device, time
  • User's typical behavior patterns
  • Merchant reputation and history
  • Hundreds of other features

The network learned complex fraud patterns that simple rules miss:

  • Catches 70% more fraud than rule-based systems
  • Reduces false positives (legitimate transactions wrongly blocked) by 50%
  • Saves $1B+ annually in fraud losses
Neural networks are "black boxes"‚Äîhard to explain WHY they made a decision. For regulated industries (finance, healthcare), this can be a problem. Always balance accuracy with explainability!

Training Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Vanishing Gradients

  • Problem: In deep networks, error signals get weaker as they propagate backward
  • Solution: Use ReLU activation, batch normalization, skip connections (ResNet architecture)

Challenge: Overfitting

  • Problem: Network memorizes training data instead of learning patterns
  • Solution: Dropout (randomly disable neurons during training), regularization, more data

Challenge: Slow Training

  • Problem: Training can take days or weeks on large datasets
  • Solution: Use GPUs (100x faster than CPUs), batch processing, transfer learning

When to Use Neural Networks

‚úÖ Great For:

  • Large datasets (millions of examples)
  • Complex patterns that simpler algorithms miss
  • Images, audio, video, text (unstructured data)
  • When accuracy is paramount and explainability less important

‚ùå Not Ideal For:

  • Small datasets (thousands of examples) ‚Äî will overfit
  • When you need to explain decisions to regulators/customers
  • Simple problems where linear regression or decision trees work fine
  • When training time/computational cost is limited

Deep Learning Revolution

What Makes it "Deep"?

"Deep learning" simply means neural networks with many hidden layers (typically 10+, sometimes hundreds!). More layers = ability to learn more complex, abstract patterns.

üìä Deep learning now powers 90%+ of breakthrough AI applications (up from <10% in 2012)

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs): Masters of Vision

Architecture:

  • Convolutional Layers: Scan image with filters to detect features (edges, corners, textures)
  • Pooling Layers: Reduce image size while keeping important features (makes processing faster)
  • Fully Connected Layers: Make final decision based on detected features

Real Application: Tesla Autopilot

  • 8 cameras feed images to CNNs
  • First layers: Detect edges, lines, shapes
  • Middle layers: Recognize cars, pedestrians, lane markings, traffic signs
  • Final layers: Decide steering angle, acceleration, braking
  • Processes 2,000+ frames per second for real-time driving

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs): Understanding Sequences

What's Special: RNNs have "memory"‚Äîthey remember previous inputs in the sequence, making them perfect for time series and language.

Architecture:

  • Each neuron not only processes current input but also receives information from previous step
  • Allows network to understand context (the word "bank" means different things in "river bank" vs "bank account")

Real Application: Google Translate

  • Reads sentence word-by-word, building understanding of meaning
  • Handles variable-length sentences (some sentences are short, some long)
  • Maintains context across entire sentence, not just individual words
  • Supports 133 languages with 90%+ accuracy for common pairs

Improved Version: LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)

  • Solves RNN problem of forgetting old information in long sequences
  • Has special "gates" that decide what to remember and what to forget
  • Used in voice assistants (Siri, Alexa) and predictive text

Transformers: The Latest Revolution

What Changed: Transformers replaced RNNs for language tasks. They're faster, more accurate, and scale better.

Key Innovation: Attention Mechanism

  • Instead of processing words one-by-one, looks at entire sentence at once
  • "Pays attention" to relevant words (e.g., in "The cat sat on the mat", "cat" and "sat" are related)
  • Can process much longer sequences than RNNs (thousands of words vs hundreds)

Real Application: ChatGPT (GPT = Generative Pre-trained Transformer)

  • Based on transformer architecture with 175 billion parameters
  • Trained on hundreds of billions of words from internet
  • Learned patterns in how humans write and reason
  • Can generate coherent, contextually relevant responses on any topic

Why Deep Learning Exploded After 2012

Three Key Enablers:

  1. Big Data: Internet created massive labeled datasets (billions of images, text examples)
  2. GPUs: Graphics cards (originally for video games) turned out perfect for parallel neural network computations‚Äî100x faster than CPUs
  3. Algorithmic Improvements: Better activation functions (ReLU), normalization techniques, optimizers (Adam), architectures (ResNet, Transformers)

Breakthrough Moment: 2012 ImageNet competition‚Äîdeep CNN (AlexNet) crushed traditional algorithms, reducing error rate from 26% to 16%

Transfer learning is a game-changer! Instead of training from scratch (takes weeks), start with a pre-trained network (like ResNet, BERT) and fine-tune it for your specific task. Reduces training time from weeks to hours!

  • Manufacturing: Computer vision inspects products 10x faster than humans with 99%+ accuracy ‚Üí Fewer defects, lower costs

Random Forest

Wisdom of the Crowd

Random Forest creates hundreds or thousands of decision trees, each trained on different random subsets of data. Then they all vote on the answer. It's like asking 100 experts instead of one!

How It Works

  1. Bootstrap: Create multiple datasets by randomly sampling from original data (with replacement)
  2. Train Trees: Build a decision tree on each dataset, using only random subset of features at each split
  3. Vote: For classification, each tree votes; majority wins. For regression, average all predictions.

Business Application: Credit Scoring at Lending Club

Lending Club uses Random Forest to assess loan risk:

  • Each tree looks at different aspects: some focus on income, others on credit history, others on loan purpose
  • Trees vote: "approve" vs "deny"
  • If 70%+ trees say "approve" ‚Üí Loan approved
  • Result: 15% better default prediction than single decision tree, $50M in prevented losses
Random Forests are the "Swiss Army knife" of machine learning. When you don't know which algorithm to try first, Random Forest is often a safe bet‚Äîworks well on most problems without much tuning!

Advantages

  • More accurate than single decision trees
  • Handles large datasets with many features
  • Automatically handles missing values
  • Provides feature importance rankings (which inputs matter most)
  • Resistant to overfitting

Support Vector Machines

Drawing the Best Boundary

SVM finds the line (or hyperplane in higher dimensions) that best separates different categories, maximizing the distance to the nearest points from each class. Think of it as finding the "safest" boundary with the widest margin.

Imagine separating red and blue marbles on a table. You could draw many lines to separate them. SVM finds the line with the most empty space on both sides‚Äîthe "safest" separator that's least likely to misclassify points near the boundary.

Business Application: Text Classification

Email Spam Filtering:

  • Each email represented as point in high-dimensional space (each word/phrase = dimension)
  • SVM finds boundary separating spam from legitimate emails
  • New email: Check which side of boundary it falls on
  • Accuracy: 98%+ for well-trained systems

The Kernel Trick

SVMs can handle non-linear boundaries using the "kernel trick":

  • Project data into higher dimensions where linear separation is possible
  • Like lifting 2D data into 3D where you can slice through it with a plane
  • Common kernels: Linear, Polynomial, RBF (Radial Basis Function)

When to Use SVM

‚úÖ Great for: Text classification, image recognition (before deep learning), small-to-medium datasets with many features

‚ùå Avoid for: Very large datasets (slow to train), when probability estimates are needed (SVM gives classification only)

Naive Bayes

Probability-Based Classification

Naive Bayes uses probability theory: "Given that this email contains the words 'FREE', 'CLICK HERE', and 'WINNER', what's the probability it's spam?" It's based on Bayes' Theorem from statistics.

Why "Naive"?

It makes a "naive" assumption: all features are independent of each other. In reality, features often relate (if email has "FREE", it's more likely to also have "CLICK HERE"). Despite this unrealistic assumption, Naive Bayes works surprisingly well in practice!

Business Application: Customer Sentiment Analysis

Analyzing Product Reviews:

  • Training: Learn probability that words appear in positive vs. negative reviews
  • New review: "This product is amazing and fast!"
  • Calculate: P(positive | "amazing", "fast") vs P(negative | "amazing", "fast")
  • Prediction: Positive sentiment (higher probability)
  • Speed: Can classify thousands of reviews per second
Naive Bayes is incredibly fast and works well with small training datasets. Perfect for quick prototypes and real-time classification where speed matters more than perfect accuracy!

Advantages

  • Extremely fast to train and predict
  • Works well with small datasets
  • Handles high-dimensional data well
  • Provides probability estimates (not just classifications)
  • Simple to implement and understand

K-Means Clustering

Unsupervised Learning: Finding Natural Groups

K-Means is different from other algorithms we've discussed‚Äîit's unsupervised, meaning it doesn't need labeled training data. It just looks at your data and finds natural groupings!

How K-Means Works

  1. Choose K: Decide how many clusters you want (e.g., K=3 for 3 customer segments)
  2. Initialize: Randomly place K "centroids" (cluster centers)
  3. Assign: Put each data point in the cluster of its nearest centroid
  4. Update: Move each centroid to the average position of points in its cluster
  5. Repeat: Steps 3-4 until centroids stop moving

Business Application: Customer Segmentation

E-commerce Example:

  • Input Data: Customer purchase frequency, average order value, product categories bought, time since last purchase
  • K-Means with K=4 discovers:
    • Cluster 1: High-value, frequent buyers (VIPs)
    • Cluster 2: Occasional buyers, moderate spending (Regulars)
    • Cluster 3: One-time purchasers, low value (At-risk)
    • Cluster 4: Inactive accounts (Churned)
  • Action: Target each segment with appropriate messaging‚ÄîVIP perks for Cluster 1, win-back offers for Cluster 3

More Applications

  • Image Compression: Cluster similar colors together, reducing file size
  • Anomaly Detection: Points far from any cluster = outliers = potential fraud/defects
  • Document Organization: Group similar articles/emails without pre-defined categories
  • Market Research: Identify distinct consumer segments from survey data
How to choose K? Try the "elbow method": run K-Means with K=1, 2, 3, ... 10. Plot within-cluster variation vs. K. Look for the "elbow" where adding more clusters doesn't help much‚Äîthat's your optimal K!

Limitations

  • Must specify K upfront (doesn't tell you how many clusters exist)
  • Sensitive to initial centroid placement (run multiple times with different starting points)
  • Assumes clusters are spherical and similar size
  • Struggles with clusters of different densities or odd shapes
